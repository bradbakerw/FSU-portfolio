Music as a discipline

For many years music was considered among the Math and Science subjects. It is only a relatively recent phenomenon in history that music has branched into being seen more as an art.

Musicians use many parts of their brain simultaneously. While a musician is performing, they are continually counting rhythm, calculating note lengths, visually and audibly observing their surroundings to connect and syncronize their sound with fellow performers, and making artistic and cretive decisions about their own playing. In addition, they are relying on muscle memory to press the right buttons, slide to the right positions, or manipulate their oral shape correctly to produce the desired sound on their instrument or voice.

Music is a growing and changing discipline. Though some aspects of music seem to be well established in many years of history, the artistic nature of it demands for itself a freedom to grow and change as each rising musician creatively inputs their ideas through composition, performance, critique. The term "Music Theory" suggests the fluidity of the structure of music, because though it's well established, there is no necessarily right or wrong way to do it. This makes for subjective evaltuation of quality, and a wide variety of taste and preference. We can see this represented today by the diverse and still further diversifying selection of music available in the media.
